00 · The Cutting Principle

The edit is the breath.

An animatic is the film with the courage held in. Every cut here was decided before the shoot — not because we don't trust the editor, but because the rhythm IS the message. The film breathes in for 18 seconds, holds for 2, exhales for 40. Anything that breaks that breath gets cut.

Eleven hard cuts. One morph. Two seconds of silence that decide whether the film works at all.

04 · Hero Moments

The three cuts that decide everything.

Three moments in the film must be perfect or the whole thing collapses. Each gets a frame-by-frame breakdown — eight frames sampled across the moment with audio sync notes. Print these and pin them above the editor's monitor.

Hero Moment 01 · The Silence

The transition from the server-wall thesis into the gold paper is the breath of the film. Hold the final server frame for 24 frames after the last word lands — long enough that the audience starts to wonder if the projector has stopped. Then cut to black. Twelve frames of pure black silence. No sound, no music, no ambience. The room you're in becomes the soundtrack.

At 00:00:18:12, the gold paper enters frame top — soundlessly at first. At 00:00:19:00, the single soprano enters on a sustained vowel. This is the only place in the film where music STARTS rather than continues. The audience has been holding their breath for two seconds; the soprano is the exhale.

If your test screening reports that this gap "felt too long" — leave it. That feeling is the work. If they don't notice the silence at all, you cut it short. Add 12 more frames.

The water-touch at 00:00:23:18 must be frame-accurate to the soprano's vocal entry. The paper hits, the ripple spreads, the sound enters — three events on the same frame. The composer cuts to picture, not the other way around.

The dissolve from paper to origami crane (24:00 → 24:12) is the only soft transition in the film. Use a luminance-matched dissolve, not a straight cross-fade — the gold of the paper should bleed into the gold of the crane without a colour shift. If you can't tell where one ends and the other begins, you've done it right.

The water SFX is a single "tup" — recorded with a finger on still water in a glass bowl. Do NOT use a stock library splash. Stock splashes have presence; this needs absence with a single point of contact.

The brightest gold node in shot 10 (centre, frame 47:23) and the centre of the iris in shot 11 (frame 48:00) must occupy the same pixel coordinates. Not approximately. Exactly. The audience's pupil tracks the bright point — if it moves on the cut, the magic dies.

The cut itself is on a single frame: 00:00:48:00. The gold node fills frame, then in one frame becomes the iris of the surveillance system. The villain has returned — but it is now small, dim, and blind. The audience understands without being told that the favour world has overwhelmed it.

Audio across the cut: the choir's last sustained note from shot 10 cuts hard against a single signal-lost beep on the same frame. One frame of audio overlap — the two worlds collide and one wins. Mix the beep at -18LUFS so it feels small. The choir was full. The system is now a whisper.

05 · Text Timing

Read time, not screen time.

Average reader = 4 words/second silent, 2.5 words/second comprehending. Every text card is timed for the slower number, then held an extra 12 frames. The audience must finish reading before the cut, or the cut robs the line.

07 · Editor's Notes

Five rules. Print these.

  1. 01 Defend the silence at 00:00:18:00. Every test screening will tell you to shorten it. Don't. The film fails without it.
  2. 02 Pixel-lock the match-cut at 00:00:48:00. If the centres are off by even 4px, the magic dies. Use a guide overlay during conform.
  3. 03 Never sweeten the cuts. No whoosh, no riser, no impact. Eleven of twelve transitions are hard cuts. The discomfort IS the form.
  4. 04 Cut the music to picture, never the other way. If a word lands wrong, move the picture. The cello and choir are the spine — bend the visuals around them.
  5. 05 End at 00:01:00:00. No URL repeat card. No legal bug. No "now available." The film ends where it ends. Trust the audience to type the URL themselves.
